The U.S. Constitution establishes the relationship between the national and state governments. This chart shows you examples of the delegated, reserved, and concurrent powers.

Delegated to the National Government Concurrent, or Shared, Powers Reserved to the State Governments
The power to:
  • organize and control trade between states and with other nations
  • coin and print money
  • make agreements with foreign nations
  • establish post offices and interstate highways
  • raise and support military forces
  • declare war and make peace
  • govern U.S. territories and admit new states
  • create rules and laws for people who wish to move into the nation and how they may become citizens
 The power to:
  • raise and collect taxes
  • borrow money
  • establish courts
  • grant permission to start banks
  • enforce laws and punish lawbreakers
  • provide health care services and other assistance to the people
 The power to:
  • create local government
  • set up businesses
  • build and support public schools
  • organize and control trade within the state
  • conduct elections
  • determine rules for voting, such as living in the district where the person votes
  • make marriage laws
  • determine the requirements for professional workers, like doctors and teachers
