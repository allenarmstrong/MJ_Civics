What Were the Weaknesses of the Articles of Confederation?

The Articles of Confederation lasted only 10 years. The Founding Fathers made the first U.S. government weak to prevent tyranny. However, the weaknesses created many problems. Study these flip cards to learn more about the problems.

Each state had one vote in the legislative branch.

States with larger populations, like Virginia, did not think it was fair to have the same amount of power as a state with fewer people.

The government did not have an executive branch.

When Congress passed a law, the state governments were supposed to enforce it. Congress had no power to make sure each state enforced the law.

The government did not have a separate judicial branch.

If a law's meaning was in question, there was no one who could fairly settle the issue and make sure all states followed the ruling.

Congress could not create taxes.

Taxes pay for a government's functions. Congress could only request money from the state legislatures. If the states did not send any, Congress was powerless.

Congress could not raise a national military separate from the states.

During the Revolutionary War, General George Washington had to request money for supplies. Congress could not force the states to pay for the troops' needs, and they often went without, starving at times.

Each state printed its own currency.

A person might need paper money from several different states to do business. Currencies from different states did not have the same value.

Congress could not control trade between states and with other nations.

Each state acted for its own benefit and did not consider the good of the whole nation. Other nations could take advantage of differences in the states' trade agreements.

New laws would require the support of nine of the 13 states, more than a majority.

Congress had a difficult time passing laws because nearly all of the states would have to agree. Its work was limited as a result.

The Articles of Confederation required unanimous agreement of the states to amend the document.

Congress realized that the Articles of Confederation needed changes. Yet it could do nothing without all the states' support. Unanimous agreement is rare, even in everyday situations.
