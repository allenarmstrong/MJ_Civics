How Do State Governments Make Laws?

State governments are set up similar to the federal government. Most state legislaturesState Legislatures: the part of a state government responsible for making laws are bicameralBicameral: a part of government that makes laws, consisting of two houses. Each chamber of the state legislature is similar in structure to the House and the Senate in Congress.

A citizen or group of citizens can suggest a new law. A state senator or representative officially introduces the idea. At the state level, just like the federal level, most of the work on bills happens in committees. Another similarity is that both chambers of the legislature must pass the same bill. Then they send the approved bill to the governorGovernor: the top executive of a state government, elected by the residents of that state.

The governor, like the president, has three choices. The governor can sign the bill into law, vetoVeto: the power of the U.S. president to reject a law passed by Congress the bill, or take no action (in Florida, a passed bill with no action from the governor becomes law in 15 days).

In the Florida legislature, the Staff Analysis may be one of the most important documents in the lawmaking process. Once a bill goes into committee, a Staff Analysis is conducted. The Staff Analysis is a report that the committee working on the bill produces for the rest of the legislature to review. The Staff Analysis describes the bill and its differences from existing laws. It may also describe how the bill may affect the state budget.
