Justice Elevator Text Version

Instructions: You are a clerk in the federal court system. Help citizens find their way to the right court for the next step in their cases by answering questions. All new cases begin at the trial court level. A client arrives in your office! The client is ready for the first hearing of a federal trial.

Note: Glossary terms are defined at the end of the text version.

U.S. Supreme Court

  • highest court in the United States
  • only court created by the U.S. Constitution
  • reviews select cases from a U.S. Court of Appeals or a state-level supreme court
  • hears no new evidence
  • usually a case gets to this level because people question whether a certain law violates the Constitution
  • nine justices
  • court issues a majority opinion (decision of the majority of justices)
  • majority opinion can affirm the constitutionality of a law or declare it unconstitutional
  • justices who do not vote with the majority issue a dissenting, or minority, opinion to explain why they disagree

U.S. Court of Appeals

  • reviews select cases from a U.S. District Court
  • usually a three-judge panel hears the case
  • no new evidence presented
  • at least one U.S. Court of Appeals in each of the 12 circuits
  • court can affirm the lower court decision (“let it stand”), reverse the decision, or send the case back to the lower court

U.S. District Court

  • first court to hear a federal case
  • jury can decide the case
  • criminal and civil cases
  • includes cases involving more than one state
  • each side presents evidence to prove their argument
  • 94 districts organized into 12 circuits
  • at least one U.S. District Court in each state

Glossary Terms

Affirm: when an appellate court decides the lower court made the correct decision
Dissenting opinion: the explanation behind the judgment of supreme court justices who did not agree with the majority opinion, also known as the minority opinion
Evidence: objects or information used in court to prove the facts of the case
Federal: referring to a system of government under federalism or to the central government of a nation under this system
Jury: a group of ordinary citizens who decide together whether a person accused of a crime is guilty or not guilty
Majority opinion: the judicial opinion agreed to by more than half of the members of a court
Minority opinion: the judicial opinion of the judges or justices who did not agree with the majority opinion, also known as the dissenting opinion
Trial court: a court in which a case is heard and decided for the first time
Unconstitutional: not allowed by or against the ideas and principles of the Constitution
