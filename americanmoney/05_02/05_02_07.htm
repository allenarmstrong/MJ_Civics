How Do Businesses Shape Society?

As businesses grow and thrive, they can become important parts of our everyday lives. You may have a favorite place for French fries or having fun. Perhaps you are loyal to a brand of shoes. The goods and services we use are part of American culture. Growth makes a business familiar to more and more people, to the point where you can name a business and no one asks what you mean. For example, people around the world as well as Americans know what McDonald's® is and what is sold there.

In this way, business growth encourages cultural diffusion. People share the common experience of certain goods and services. As a business grows and opens locations in new places, that shared experience spreads to more people. 1978-The First Ben and Jerry's®

Ben Cohen and Jerry Greenfield took a course on ice-cream making. They decided to open their own ice cream shop to earn a living. Since, they figured, young people love ice cream, they chose a place that had a college to open their first shop—Burlington, Vermont.

1980-Packing and Distributing

The ice cream was already growing in popularity. The entrepreneurs began packing their ice cream into containers and delivering them to restaurants. Grocery stores and other shops begin carrying the ice cream for sale. Now people could enjoy the ice cream at home!

1981-Second Ice Cream Shop

Ben and Jerry's® opens its second ice cream shop in Shelburne, Vermont. As more locations open, more people are familiar with the ice cream. They tell their friends and families. Demand for the ice cream allows the business to expand to more areas.

1983—First Out-of-State Ice Cream Shop

Now folks in Maine could walk into a Ben and Jerry's® shop. This was the first shop opened outside of the company's home state of Vermont. Each new shop leads to a new group of employees to run the shop as well as people to make the ice cream. The company purchases the milk and cream from farmers in the area.

1985—Moving Out of the Region!

With sales now over nine million dollars per year, the ice cream is available in grocery stores in other regions of the United States besides the Northeast. In this same year, the company founded the Ben and Jerry's Foundation. It uses a portion of the company's profits to pay for community projects. The foundation continues to support efforts to improve the common good.

1986—Opens New Plant for Tours

Ben and Jerry's® opens a new ice-cream making plant in Waterbury, Vermont. You can even take tours and see how they make the ice cream! The new plant allows the company to produce even more ice cream to sell in its shops and stores nationwide. Two years later, Ben and Jerry's® would have stores in 18 U.S. states and Canada.

Today—Activism and Expansion

In the years since, Ben and Jerry's® has opened shops in most of the U.S. states as well as in other countries. They have continued their philanthropy work as well as activism in environmental issues. For example, they developed a specific flavor to support rainforest preservation using profits from sales of the new flavor. The growth of the business and company's civic action have made Ben and Jerry's® familiar to Americans and people around the world.


It's important to note that cultural diffusion is not limited to businesses and their products. Aspects of culture such as language, religion, even political movements can and do spread as people and ideas and people move and connect. Technology like television and the Internet make cultural diffusion in today's world more rapid than ever!
