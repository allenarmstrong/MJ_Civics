Justice Elevator: Answers Text Version

Instructions: You are a clerk in the federal court system. Help citizens find their way to the right court for the next step in their cases by answering questions. All new cases begin at the trial court level. A client arrives in your office! The client is ready for the first hearing of a federal trial.

Note: Glossary terms are defined at the end of the text version.

U.S. Supreme Court

  • highest court in the United States
  • only court created by the U.S. Constitution
  • reviews select cases from a U.S. Court of Appeals or a state-level supreme court
  • hears no new evidence
  • usually a case gets to this level because people question whether a certain law violates the Constitution
  • nine justices
  • court issues a majority opinion (decision of the majority of justices)
  • majority opinion can affirm the constitutionality of a law or declare it unconstitutional
  • justices who do not vote with the majority issue a dissenting, or minority, opinion to explain why they disagree

Questions

  1. If the client loses the case in this court,
    1. the decision is final and may not be appealed
    2. the state will begin a new trial
    3. the justices create a new law
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! The U.S. Supreme Court is the highest court in the U.S. court system.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! The Supreme Court has the final word, so the case will not begin again. Supreme Court justices do not make laws. They determine what the laws mean.”
  2. Which court has the final authority over all other U.S. courts?
    1. a state’s supreme court
    2. a municipal supreme court
    3. the U.S. Supreme Court
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! The U.S. Supreme Court has final authority over all other courts in the nation.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! The U.S. Supreme Court has the final authority over all other courts.”
  3. The U.S. Constitution created the
    1. State-level courts
    2. U.S. Supreme Court
    3. U.S. District Court
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! The U.S. Constitution created only the U.S. Supreme Court. Yet it does also protect the right of states to create their own court systems.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! The U.S. Constitution only created the highest court in the nation.”
  4. When a case goes to the U.S. Supreme Court
    1. all of the original witnesses must appear
    2. the president will issue the majority opinion
    3. no new evidence is allowed
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! The U.S. Supreme Court will only review evidence from the prior trials, not new information.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! The original witnesses have already been questioned. The justices will learn what they said. The president may not be a part of the decision.”
  5. If a law is labeled “unconstitutional,” that means that the U.S. Supreme Court
    1. decided that a state or federal law goes against the U.S. Constitution
    2. supports a law even though it does not appear in the U.S. Constitution
    3. will rewrite a law so that everyone can agree to it
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! The U.S. Supreme Court determines whether laws violate the U.S. Constitution.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! Most laws do not appear in the U.S. Constitution. The Supreme Court may not rewrite laws.”
  6. When the U.S. Supreme Court presents their decision,
    1. all nine justices must agree to what it says
    2. a justice who disagrees presents a dissenting opinion
    3. the justices declare that a law is unconstitutional
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! The final ruling, or majority opinion, only requires a majority of justices to agree to it. Justices who disagree present a dissenting opinion.” Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! Not every justice must agree, but a majority will. The final decision is called the “majority opinion.” The justices who did not agree may present their own opinion on the case.”
  7. Which of the following could be the last court to hear a case before it arrives at the U.S. Supreme Court?
    1. U.S. District Court
    2. U.S. Municipal Court
    3. U.S. Court of Appeals
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! The U.S. Supreme Court hears select cases drawn from the U.S. Court of Appeals and state-level supreme courts.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! The U.S. Supreme Court only hears cases that have been heard at other levels. The justices study whether the verdict is appropriate under the U.S. Constitution.”
  8. Which of the following cases is the U.S. Supreme Court most likely to hear?
    1. Citizens sue their neighbor for property damage
    2. a state prosecutes  suspects in a murder trial
    3. citizens complain that a law in their home state violates their rights
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! In this case, the constitutionality of a law is in question. This is the most likely type of case to get to the U.S. Supreme Court.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! Cases between citizens likely go to a local court. State courts handle murder cases.”

Good work! Unfortunately, the citizen lost the case. Help the citizen appeal the verdict. Please escort this citizen to the U.S. Court of Appeals to hear the case.

U.S. Court of Appeals

  • reviews select cases from a U.S. District Court
  • usually a three-judge panel hears the case
  • no new evidence presented
  • at least one U.S. Court of Appeals in each of the 12 circuits
  • court can affirm the lower court decision (“let it stand”), reverse the decision, or send the case back to the lower court

Questions

  1. A U.S. Court of Appeals is for people who want to
    1. bring a new case to trial
    2. complain about a family member’s trial
    3. challenge a lower court’s decision
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! A client who appeals is challenging a lower court’s decision.”
    Feedback for incorrect response—“Oops! New cases are heard in U.S. District Courts. Only the person who received the verdict may appeal the case.”
  2. If the events of the case happened in Texas, the people will appeal the case to a U.S. Court of Appeals for the
    1. Texas Circuit
    2. Washington, D.C., Circuit
    3. 5th Circuit
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! Texas is part of the 5th Circuit. Each circuit contains multiple states.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! Texas is one state that is part of a larger federal circuit.”
  3. Which of the following cases could a U.S. Court of Appeals select to hear?
    1. citizen who challenges the decision of a state trial court
    2. citizen who challenges the decision of the U.S. Supreme Court
    3. citizen who challenges the decision of a federal trial court
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! The U.S. Court of Appeals can review select cases from a federal trial court, called a U.S. District Court.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! A state trial case would go to that state’s supreme court. The U.S. Supreme Court is the highest court in the nation.”
  4. A U.S. District Court is a type of
    1. appeals court
    2. trial court
    3. supreme court
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! A U.S. Court of Appeals is a type of appellate court. These courts will review select cases from U.S. District Courts.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! A U.S. District Court is not a trial court. Also, it is not the highest court, so it is not a supreme court.”
  5. If the U.S. Court of Appeals affirms a case, that means that
    1. the verdict of the lower court will remain
    2. the verdict of the lower court is reversed
    3. the verdict of the lower court is unclear and needs a new trial
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! If a U.S. Court of Appeals affirms a verdict, it is saying that the decision of the lower court is correct and will remain in place.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! Affirming a verdict is saying that it was the correct verdict.”
  6. The U.S. Court of Appeals is the lowest level of federal court where
    1. a civil case may be brought to trial
    2. each side of the case may present new evidence
    3. a judge can overturn a lower court’s ruling
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! A U.S. Court of Appeals may overturn a ruling of a U.S. District Court in both criminal and civil cases. The judges will not hear new evidence, however.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! A U.S. Court of Appeals does not hear new cases or evidence.”
  7. Which of the following events would happen first?
    1. appeal
    2. trial
    3. verdict
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! First, the case would go to trial and receive a verdict. Either side of the case may appeal the verdict.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! A trial must happen before a verdict can be reached. Either side of the case may appeal the verdict.”
  8. Which of the following usually determines the decision in a U.S. Court of Appeals?
    1. panel of judges
    2. jury members
    3. citizen panel
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! A panel of three judges usually hears cases and determines verdicts in a U.S. Court of Appeals.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! A jury of citizens may determine cases in lower courts, not in the U.S. Court of Appeals.”

Good work! Unfortunately, the citizen lost the case. Help the citizen appeal the decision. Please escort this citizen to the U.S. District Court hear the case.

U.S. District Court

  • first court to hear a federal case
  • jury can decide the case
  • criminal and civil cases
  • includes cases involving more than one state
  • each side presents evidence to prove their argument
  • 94 districts organized into 12 circuits
  • at least one U.S. District Court in each state

Questions

  1. If a client loses the case in this court, a lawyer may appeal the decision to 
    1. a different U.S. District Court
    2. the U.S. Court of Appeals
    3. the U.S. Supreme Court
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! The U.S. Court of Appeals could elect to review the case next.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! A case would have to go to a different, higher court. The U.S. Supreme Court would be the final court. It is not the next one.”
  2. If the events of the case happened in Florida, the people will appear in court in
    1. Florida
    2. Washington, D.C.
    3. any U.S. District Court in the 5th Circuit
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! A U.S. District Court in Florida would hear the case.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! Each state has at least one U.S. District Court. Cases are heard in the state where the events occur.”
  3. A person accused of breaking a law would first have a
    1. civil trial
    2. appeal trial
    3. criminal trial
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! Criminal trials are for people accused of breaking laws.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! A civil trial is for problems between citizens. An appeal is for clients who challenge the trial verdict.”
  4. A U.S. District Court is a type of
    1. court of appeal
    2. trial court
    3. supreme court
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! A U.S. District Court is a type of trial court. These courts will hear civil and criminal trials related to federal law.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! A U.S. District Court hears federal cases for the first time. Clients may appeal the verdicts of this court.”
  5. Which of the following cases could a U.S. District Court select to hear?
    1. Justice City resident accused of robbing local stores
    2. Justice City resident accused of robbing stores in several counties
    3. Justice City resident accused of robbing stores in several states
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! The federal court system can hear cases involving multiple states.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! Local and cross-county cases will be heard at the state court level. A U.S. District Court is a federal court.”
  6. The U.S. District Court is the only level of federal court where
    1. a civil case may be brought to trial
    2. each side of the case may present new evidence
    3. a judge can overturn a lower court’s ruling
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! U.S. District Courts are the first to hear federal cases. Each side presents evidence to prove their arguments.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! Other federal courts may hear civil cases or overturn verdicts.”
  7. How many states have a U.S. District Court?
    1. all of them, or 50
    2. only half
    3. none, there is one U.S. District Court in Washington, D.C.
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! Each of the 50 U.S. states has at least one U.S. District Court.”
    Feedback for incorrect response—“Oops! There are 94 U.S. District Courts, with at least one court in each state.”
  8. Which of the following usually determines the decision in a U.S. District Court?
    1. judge
    2. jury
    3. justice
    Feedback for Correct Response—“Yes! Juries usually hear cases and determine verdicts in U.S. District Courts.”
    Feedback for Incorrect Response—“Oops! A group of judges or justices determine cases at the appeals or supreme court levels.”

Glossary Terms

Affirm: when an appellate court decides the lower court made the correct decision
Dissenting opinion: the explanation behind the judgment of supreme court justices who did not agree with the majority opinion, also known as the minority opinion
Evidence: objects or information used in court to prove the facts of the case
Federal: referring to a system of government under federalism or to the central government of a nation under this system
Jury: a group of ordinary citizens who decide together whether a person accused of a crime is guilty or not guilty
Majority opinion: the judicial opinion agreed to by more than half of the members of a court
Minority opinion: the judicial opinion of the judges or justices who did not agree with the majority opinion, also known as the dissenting opinion
Trial court: a court in which a case is heard and decided for the first time
Unconstitutional: not allowed by or against the ideas and principles of the Constitution
