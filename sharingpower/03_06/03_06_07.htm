How Powerful Is the U.S. Supreme Court?

Article III (Three) of the Constitution outlines the judicial branch, and gives less detail for this branch than it does for the others. As a result, the organization and powers of the federal courts has changed over time. Actions of Congress and the court's decisions have increased the power of the federal courts.

One important decision greatly increased the power of the Supreme Court. In 1803, the Supreme Court decided the case of Marbury v. Madison. The justices determined that only the Supreme Court could decide whether an act or law is a violation of the Constitution. If the justices rule that a law is unconstitutionalUnconstitutional: not allowed by or against the ideas and principles of the Constitution, that means the law is not allowed by or it is against the ideas and principles of the Constitution. The Court could clarify the meaning of a law in its decision. Through these decisions, the Supreme Court may check the power of the other branches, as well as state and local governments. However, the justices may not randomly choose laws to review. Only when someone appeals a case to the Supreme Court can this process of judicial reviewJudicial Review: the power to review and cancel laws or acts of government if determined to be unconstitutional occur.

The U.S. Supreme Court is the highest court authority in the nation. Decisions of this court are final. People who lose their cases here have nowhere to make an appeal. It is the court of last resort.
