What Are Trade-offs and Opportunity Cost?

Each time you make a choice, there are trade-offsTrade-offs: activities or items you give up in exchange for something else. For example, if you chose to purchase the sneakers, the trade-offs include the cell phone and saving for another item. A trade-off is an item you give up when you make a choice.

Opportunity costOpportunity cost: the lost experience of the most valued alternative is a bit more specific. It includes the experience that you would have had if you had chosen your next-best option. Let's say that had you not bought the sneakers, you definitely would have bought the cell phone. Your opportunity cost is the hours you would have spent playing games on and talking to friends using that cell phone.

Leaders must make choices as well. Communities and governments experience trade-offs and opportunity cost with each choice. Try the activity to see an example.

Imagine you are part of your local sports committee. Today you will help organize a soccer tournament in your area. You will make choices that lead to trade-offs and opportunity cost.

Does selecting this option result in any trade-offs?

The options you do not select are the trade-offs because you gave them up to select one scheduling option. A trade-off is giving up some of one thing to get some of another thing. So, let's say you chose to give 3 hours to the girls and 5 hours to the boys. You gave up some girls' games on the main field to give more to the boys.

Why would you choose that option next instead of the others?

The time and experience that everyone would have had with this option is the opportunity cost of the choice you made. The players, coaches, referees, families, and committee members would have had a different experience of the event. Many things influence the choice you make for scheduling, such as what you think is most "fair" to the players and coaches or what the other committee members say.

Conclusion

© 2011 Barry Austin Photography/ Lifesize/ Thinkstock

Making choices that affect many people is challenging! Community and government leaders must make choices like these each day. Scarcity, or limited resources, forces us to make choices between options that affect time, money, goods, and services. When we give up some of one thing in favor of another, such as more main field time for the girls than for the boys, we call that a trade-off. The experience and time of the second-best choice is called the opportunity cost of the decision.
