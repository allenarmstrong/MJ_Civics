What Are Some Examples of the Government Types?

Each country's government is different from the rest of the world. People in different places have concerns and ways of life that will not exactly match anywhere else. The government types, though, are general enough that they do describe most countries. Usually you would use more than one term to describe a government.

Try this activity to identify the governments of real countries.

Let's study a few examples of different types of government. You will see that some of these types can overlap. Read each description, and see if you can identify the types.

The United States

In the United States, the people elect leaders to represent them at all levels of government. Local, state, and national leaders make decisions on their behalf. The central government shares powers with the 50 states. It makes decisions that affect all the states though the states maintain separate control over their areas.

The USA, Mexico, and Canada


Creative Commons
Neighboring nations Mexico and Canada have similar systems to the United States. Both are federations and elect leaders. As a former part of the United Kingdom, Canada still recognizes the monarch. The British monarch chooses a governor-general for Canada who approves laws and people to fill many positions. Mexico has a president that may only serve one six-year term, while the U.S. president may serve two four-year terms.

Switzerland

In Switzerland, citizens can directly vote on national laws and changes to laws, or referendums. They can challenge the laws. The citizens have the right of initiative at the national level. For example, in 2011, Swiss voters rejected an initiative for minimum pay for all workers. Voters in some U.S. states have this power at the state level.

Switzerland has 26 "cantons," which are similar to states of the United States. The legislative branch is called the Federal Assembly. It has two sections, like the U.S. Congress. Members of the Federal Assembly elect the executive heads of departments, called the Federal Council. The seven members of this Federal Council must answer to the legislative branch.

China

In China, the Communist Party of China is the official political party. A small committee of older leaders from the party makes important decisions. The majority of Chinese leaders are members of the communist party. Decisions must follow the party's ideology. It includes control of the country's economy. However, note that in the past few decades China has made some changes in laws that allow some economic freedom. It trades more with other nations than it did years ago.

Great Britain

Great Britain, or Britain, is used to refer to the main island of the United Kingdom. It used to be a traditional monarchy. Changes in laws limit the king's or queen's powers. The monarch has little power today. He or she assigns most powers to officials of the executive branch, called ministers. Most ministers are members of the legislative branch, and they must report to it.

The legislative branch has two sections, like the U.S. Congress. It chooses a prime minister. He or she has similar duties to the U.S. president. However, the legislative branch limits the prime minister's actions. He or she also consults with the king or queen regularly. The United Kingdom includes England, Northern Ireland, Scotland, and Wales. Each has its own government structure but all are bound to the laws of the United Kingdom.

Saudi Arabia

In Saudi Arabia, the king makes all decisions. He can choose to consult other members of the royal family. Members of the royal family can become king or fill other government positions. They do not hold elections. The official religion in Saudi Arabia, Islam, is the only limit on the king's power. He decides who the members of the legislature will be and approves their decisions. He decides who his advisors will be and who will carry out the laws. Since 2005, the king of Saudi Arabia has made a few changes to make the government more modern. One example is assigning professionals who are outside the royal family to debate important national policies.
