What Are the Rules for Citizenship by Birth?

A person can be a U.S. citizen at the time of birth through two main ways. Jus soli (pronounced YOO SO-LEE) means "law of the soil." People born in U.S. states or territories are citizens because they were born on U.S. soil. Jus sanguinis (pronounced YOO SAN-GWIN-NISS) means "law of the blood." People born to at least one parent who is a U.S. citizen and has lived in the United States are citizens through their blood relationship to this parent.

Most U.S. citizen births, but not all, meet both the jus soli and jus sanguinis rules. The United States has lawsLaw: a binding or enforceable rule, or set of rules to define special situations.

For example, say a couple visiting the United States from Canada has an emergency and their baby is born in a U.S. hospital. This baby is a U.S. citizen because of jus soli. Many Americans mistakenly believe that births on U.S. military bases are covered by jus soli. The citizenship of babies born on military bases depends on jus sanguinis. At least one parent must be a U.S. citizen and meet residencyResidency: where a person lives or has lived, and for how long rules.
