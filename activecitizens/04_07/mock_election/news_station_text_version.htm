News Station Text Version

Reporter Stew Preserves: Welcome to the News Station! We have two offices here—our newspaper, The Grape Press, and our TV studio, Concord TV. Candidates use all forms of media to reach voters. Examples include television, newspapers, and the Internet. Most Americans get the news from TV. However, the Internet is great for campaigns because of its relatively low cost and high speed. Thousands or even millions of people can read a message posted on the Internet within minutes. On the other hand, the scripts for television are prepared and edited long before broadcast. TV and newspapers often have websites to stay competitive, publishing bits of news as soon as it is available. You can see the candidates’ websites at the Concord Library. Here we will examine the advertisements they place on TV and in the newspaper. Remember to take notes now to help you in the election later!

To be an informed voter, you need to learn how to view these ads with a critical eye. Advertisements, whether for a candidate or a product, are trying to convince you to do something. Creators of ads have specific characteristics that make them persuasive. If you are aware of these characteristics, you can understand the purpose of the ad. You can determine whether it makes a claim with which you either agree or disagree. Advertisements show bias (preference toward a particular belief or attitude), often use symbols (something, such as a picture, that represents something else), and are propaganda (information that an organization or government sends out to promote a policy, idea, or cause).

Bias and Propaganda
 

 Bias Symbol Propaganda
Explanation A political advertisement has bias because the maker shows preference toward a particular belief or attitude. Symbols are images or text in advertisements that represent something else or have a meaning not obvious from the picture itself. Propaganda is information an organization or government sends out to promote a policy, idea, or cause. An advertisement is a type of propaganda. Many people think of the word “propaganda” as something bad, but its original meaning is neutral. Propaganda can promote both popular and unpopular ideas.
Examples An ad for Rita Republican shows a bias toward her plans for Concord City.

An ad that points out Norman No-Tax’s lack of political experience shows a bias toward candidates serving in elected positions.

 Donkeys on David Democrat’s website are symbols for the Democratic Party, of which he is a member.

Candidate ads that use an American flag are trying to convince viewers that the candidate is proud of his or her country.

 All candidate advertisements, including websites, are examples of propaganda. People responsible for the campaigns or the candidates themselves create the advertisements to promote themselves as the best person for office.

TV Ads

View each of these television ads. Pay attention to the bias and symbolism displayed in each one. Remember that advertisements are propaganda. What action does each ad want people to take?

Ad 1

Audio Visual
Rita Republican knows the law. In her years of experience on the Concord City police force, people felt safe once again to leave doors unlocked and take walks at night. The ad shows Rita in a police officer’s uniform. The uniform is a symbol of law, order, and safety. The ad wants people to think of Rita as someone who will protect the people.
Rita knows heritage and tradition are important to Concord City. She spends time with the people, keeping the tradition alive. Rita is shown walking through a grape orchard with a farmer. Then she is at a community event.
The ad uses positive words like “heritage” and “tradition” and shows Rita spending time with others in the community. The ad wants people to think Rita values these ideas and all people in Concord City.
As mayor, Rita continued to lower crime and started a junior law program for high school students. These model young people will be the next generation of guardians of Concord City tradition. Rita is shown in a professional suit speaking to a group of students in police uniforms (without all the trappings) and an American flag in the background.
Showing Rita in a professional suit reminds viewers of her success as mayor in her first term. Emphasizing her work with youth appeals to parents and community leaders.
Keep heritage and tradition alive in Concord City. Stick with experience. Vote for Rita Republican on Election Day. Close up of Rita Republican smiling with a bright background that includes a waving American flag and a Concord City flag.
The American flag appears as a symbol of pride in the nation. The words “heritage” and “tradition” repeat to appeal to voter’s emotions. “Stick with experience” is a catch phrase that will stick in the minds of viewers.

Ad 2

Audio Visual
In the last four years, the number of high school graduates in Concord City rose by 30%. More students are choosing to attend college or job training programs. Test scores of our elementary and middle school students show higher gains than in years past. David Democrat is shown at a high school graduation ceremony, and in the classroom among elementary-aged students.
Starting the ad with positive bits of information about the city’s children will grab voters’ attention. Showing David Democrat among the students is sending a symbolic message that he is responsible for these great changes.
David Democrat knows the importance of education to our children. The children of Concord City deserve the very best education we can give them. He knows the future of our city depends on it. That’s why David Democrat serves on the school board, making changes in the best interest of our schools. More images of David with students, then professional dress and speaking at a school board meeting.
The ad uses glittering generalities, meaning phrases that sound great but have little meaning behind them. For example, “best education” appeals to voters, especially parents, but does not say what specific changes David helped make to give students the “best education.”
David made great strides on the school board, and now he will be the best representative for all the people of Concord City. Put education and the future of Concord City first. Vote for David Democrat—he’s the best. David is shown in his university cap and gown, smiling and shaking hands with many people, then close up of David smiling with classroom paraphernalia behind him
The ad makes repeated use of the word “best” to impress viewers that David is the best choice for mayor. Showing David in his own college graduation robes emphasizes his qualifications. The school-related items in the background are symbols for education and learning.

Ad 3

Audio Visual
Progress is the perfect grape. Elaine Environs knows perfect grapes. A bunch of fresh grapes being washed with a bright sunny background, then Elaine Environs slides into the screen smiling at the grapes.
The grapes are a symbol for the major industry of Concord City. The ad appeals to farmers and other voters who make their living from the grapes. A bounty of grapes washed with plenty of water gives the viewer an image of a healthy crop, not the lack of rainfall the city has had lately.
(Elaine Environs speaking) “I will bring the perfect grape to Concord City. My new ideas will secure the grape crop and the future you want. A different future than Rita Republican can offer—one where the grass is still green and lush.” Close up of Elaine dressed professionally speaking these words with a background of gentle rain falling on a lush, healthy grape orchard.
The ad uses Elaine speaking directly to the people to show her confidence in her words. The ad distinguishes Elaine through the propaganda technique of “new and different.” Elaine’s ideas are new, different, and better than those of Rita Republican. Viewers will feel she is making a promise to them, and the background gives them a mental image of a bright future with her.
Elaine Environs knows how to make the perfect grape. She knows how to make our city shine again. Vote for Elaine Environs on Election Day. Elaine dressed in work clothes composting, tending grapevines, working with students in the school orchard.
“Progress is the perfect grape” is Elaine Environs’ catch phrase. Progress suggests change for the better. Showing Elaine working with the grapes and with students emphasizes her experience growing grapes. It is designed to make farmers believe she can help them prosper again.

Ad 4

Audio Visual
(Silence) Words on screen, black and white—“Don’t let the city steal any more of YOUR money. Leaders who have never worked the grape orchard are useless. They have no idea of the challenges we face.”
The stark contrast of black and white words on the screen and no sound grabs viewers’ attention. They see the word “your” emphasized. The words and arrangement make people feel they have been wronged, that their rights were violated. It uses a propaganda technique called “loaded words.” Saying that non-farmers in office are “useless” is designed to make voters feel negative about the other candidates.
(Norman No-Tax speaking) As mayor, I will make sure you keep more of your money. I will lower taxes in Concord City to give farmers the fair chance they deserve!” Norman No-Tax speaking these words in a grape orchard, dressed as though working himself at the time.
Norman No-Tax’s appearance in a grape orchard reminds voters that he is a farmer too, like many of them. It suggests that he too knows the struggles farmers face in growing the crop and paying their bills, such as city taxes.
Vote for Norman No-Tax and put more money where it belongs—in YOUR pockets. Norman smiling and spending time with other farmers and their families, all appear happy, gleaming tractor in the background, table laid with a feast.
The smiling farmers, food, and new tractor all are symbols for the prosperity Norman is promising if he is mayor. The ad wants voters to believe that he will be able to lower taxes enough that farmers will have a better future in Concord City.

Print Ads

Propaganda can appear in print as well. View each page of this newspaper to see different examples of political ads. How are these different from the television advertisements?

Page 1 (front page) of Newspaper

Title of newspaper The Grape Press
Biggest headline: Campaigns Heat Up—Debate Tonight at City Hall!
Other headline: “No Rain in Forecast, Vines Dying”
Advertisement description: Shows David Democrat with grimace in black and white next to Rita Republican smiling in color Text of ad says, “David Democrat says he’s for the children, but he voted against increasing school funding last month. Our kids need technology to compete in business. Keep Rita in office on Election Day. She knows what kids really need.”
Information for ad: The ad uses black and white and a non-flattering photo of David Democrat to make him look unpleasant. Rita appears in color and smiling to send a positive message about her. Viewers know that either Rita or a group that supports her paid for the ad. The ad uses a propaganda technique called “selective facts.” It states that David voted against increasing funding, but does not state why. Perhaps there was a good reason he voted against it. The ad also suggests that the funding was for technology, but viewers cannot tell from the ad if this is true.

Page 2 of Newspaper

Headlines for separate articles :

“Citizen Meeting for Concord Field”
“New Budget Shows Promise”

Advertisement description: Ad shows a toxic wasteland, factory sludge, and similar. Text of ad says, “Is this the future you want for the children of Concord City? Protect our Earth, protect our children. Our kids beg us for no factories. Please don’t vote for Rita Republican on Election Day.”
Information for ad: The ad uses a powerfully negative and frightening image of a ruined land. It connects the image to the future of the city, children, Rita’s plan to build a factory, and voting for her. The symbolic message is that if you vote for Rita, then Concord City will become a wasteland. It appeals to people’s emotions, especially fear. Playing on emotions is a common propaganda technique.

Page 3 of Newspaper

Headlines for separate articles:

“Laptops Donated to High School”
“Experts Say School Test Scores Flawed”

Advertisement description: Shows a farm family in front of run-down home, looking sad. Text of ad is a quote, “We tried the new techniques. We trusted Elaine. However, our crops are dying like everyone else’s, actually worse. We will be lucky to keep the farm.” –Mr. Weltcher, Concord City farmer
Information for ad: This ad also uses fear. It shows a family in difficult circumstances. Their farm is failing, and they do not have enough income to maintain their property. The ad blames their situation on trying Elaine Environs new farming techniques. It intends to make all farmers in Concord City not vote for Elaine, to make them think her methods fail on real farms like that of the Weltcher’s.

Page 4 of Newspaper

Headlines for separate articles:

 “Factory Talks in Progress”
“Annual Grape Festival Cancelled?”

Advertisement description: The ad has two side-by-side images, one of broken-down farm equipment and dry orchards and Norman No-Tax in black and white, the other of a lush orchard and a clean tractor driven by a smiling farmer in color. The text of the ad reads “Concord City—the local government that has supported its farmers for generations. Support the local government; put someone in office who knows how to protect its people. Norman No-Tax is just a bunch of sour grapes.”
Information for ad: This ad associates Norman No-Tax with sour grapes and failing orchards. The message it sends voters is that Norman has no respect for the city’s government. Without mentioning his plan to lower taxes directly, it suggests that doing so will reduce support of city government for the farmers. The ad also plays on fear and uses sour grapes, dry orchards, a broken tractor, and a running tractor as symbols for the choice voters have to make.

Did you notice that each of these ads was an attack against one of the candidates? Attack ads are another common propaganda technique. People try to make a candidate look bad so people will not vote for that person. In candidate advertising, the ads tend to do one of three things. They might acclaim or celebrate a person’s successes, like the television ads. Ads can also attack another candidate or defend a candidate from an attack.
