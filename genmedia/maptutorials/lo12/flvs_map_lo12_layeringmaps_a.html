How can technology help us compare data?

Lesson 1 of 6:

Imagine you want to find out whether your community builds schools in neighborhoods with higher concentrations of residents. You plan to compare population density in your community with school locations. You find a map of schools in your area.

However, the population-density map you find shows the whole country. Your community is too small to see. You wish you could lay the maps on top of each other for easy comparison, but because the map is not transparent, it is a pointless effort.

Map Layering, which includes multiple data sets in a map for comparison, technology comes to the rescue!

Values and Limitations

Lesson 3 of 6:

Layering maps has both values and limitations for those using the applications. You should have discovered a few key points about layering maps.

(Image is a bullet list of text with the following statements)

Layered Maps:
  • are a powerful tool for learning about an area

  • allow you to easily compare data maps

  • allow you to discover relationships between different characteristics of an area

  • limit data to what you need to see, which makes the map easier to study

  • use data layers that do not cover each other or will not be visible at the same time

  • may not have all the data you need to answer a specific question and may require additional resources

Layered maps have many applications, or ways you can use the information the show. Imagine you wish to open your own business. You want to make a fun, safe place for teens to hang out with their friends. Some areas already have plenty of recreational spots available for teens. A Note on Data

Lesson 5 of 6:

Cartographers can use map layers to show quantitative and qualitative data sets. Qualitative data is something you can see and describe but not measure. Quantitative data is measurable in terms using numbers. The amount of data a cartographer could show in a map is infinite.
Remember that the cartographer will choose what data to include and how to display it so the map is useful and attractive. For example, the cartographer may generalize the data to a larger category, like showing population density by state instead of county. These choices affect whether a data layer will meet your needs.

Move over a hotspot for more information:

(Image is a map of Europe showing most of the countries which make up the continent, we can see the southern tip of Ireland and eastern border of Spain. The countries are labeled by name and borders. The Map is colored 2 different shades of green throughout the eastern and northern areas of the continent. As we move towards the center of the map, near the Mediterranean Sea, we can see that there are shades of tan, light brown, and dark brown. These colors indicate changes in elevation, possibly mountain ranges. Hot spots are located in the country of Italy, Switzerland, the Czech Republic, and Ukraine. We see coast lines bordering the northern and western areas of the map a)

Hotspot descriptions:

Italy: Qualitative; The outline of Italy resembles a boot.

Switzerland: Qualitative; Switzerland is a mountainous region. Quantitative; Switzerland has zero miles of coastline.

Czech Republic: Quantitative; The Czech Republic shares a border with four nations.

Ukraine: Qualitative; The map is simple and attractive, showing the physical features of Europe's nations.. Quantitative; The maps uses five colors to show elevation differences.
