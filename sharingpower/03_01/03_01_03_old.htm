What Is Federalism?

Some nations have a unitary governmentUnitary government: form of government in which local and state governments get their powers from a strong central government and are under the control of the central government. Most, if not all, power is in one central government in this type. Early leaders knew that the United States needed a government where power was divided and shared. The division would protect people's rights. It would also better serve the nation's needs.

Federalism is the word we use to describe the division of powers among local, state, and national governments. It is a way to spread out power and authority. Federalism prevents any one person or group from becoming too powerful.

The U.S. ConstitutionU.S. Constitution: plan detailing the structure, functions, and powers of the government of the United States, also known as the Constitution outlines the structure and organization of the central, or national, government. It lists the delegated powersDelegated powers: powers granted to the national government by the U.S. Constitution, which are powers assigned to the national government. It also includes some of the reserved powersReserved powers: powers of the state governments not given to the national government nor denied to the states, powers not given to the national government that the state governments can use. The national and state governments also share some powers, called concurrent powersConcurrent powers: powers shared by the national, state, and/or local governments.
