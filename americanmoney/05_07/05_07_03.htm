What Is Credit?

Most people think of credit cards when you say "credit." In fact, many types of credit exist. Besides cards, credit can be in the form of a loan, or a credit account through a bank or store. No matter what type or form of credit a person uses, it represents money owed.

Money from an item purchased on credit must come from somewhere. The bank, store, or other company that offered the credit charges a fee to cover a person's debtDebt: an amount of money, service, or other property owed to someone until they pay it back. We call this fee interestInterest: rate paid or charged for use of money. If a person does not pay off the credit debt right away, interest adds to the debt and it grows over time. The longer a person takes to pay off the debt, the more interest they will pay, such as the student who bought the TV on credit.

Many Americans obtain bank loans to buy very expensive items like cars and homes. A mortgageMortgage: a loan for purchasing a home or property is a special type of credit specific to buying homes and property. College students can apply for student loans that have low interest fees. Credit can be securedSecured: a type of credit where you pay a deposit and can only use card up to that amount or unsecuredUnsecured: a type of credit that does not require a deposit and the lender sets the balance limit. A person who has an excellent record of paying off debt on time can qualify for unsecured credit.

Study this chart for a summary of the types of credit.

Type of Credit Explanation

Service Credit


© 2011 AP Images
 A household pays a deposit to receive a service, such as electricity. You pay a fee each month based on how much of the service you use. For example, if people in your house take long showers, your water bill will likely be higher than a family that limits time in the shower.

Loan


© 2011 AP Images
 This is a way to borrow cash, usually through a bank or credit union. People often get loans to start or expand businesses or remodel their homes.

Installment Credit


© 2011 AP Images
 This type of credit is usually for expensive items. It allows you to purchase a good or service and pay it back monthly in fixed amounts. People use this type of credit to purchase furniture, electronics, appliances, mortgages, and student loans.

Credit Card


© 2011 AP Images
 These are plastic cards with a magnetic strip issued by stores, banks, and businesses used to purchase goods and services. Depending on the person's history with paying off credit, the interest fee and limit on spending will vary.
