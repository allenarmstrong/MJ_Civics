What Are the Limits to Free Speech?

The First Amendment protects most speech or expression, but not all. Though Americans have freedom of expression, they may not use that freedom in a way that harms the rights of others. A commonly given example is yelling "fire" in a crowded theater. If there truly is a fire, that's fine. However, if it was a false cry and people are hurt in their rush to get out, the person who yelled it may be in trouble with the law. The person put other people's safety at risk for no reason.

Many limits to rights relate to public safety. For example, people have the right to assembly. However, to protect safety on the roads and free flow of traffic, local governments usually require a permit for events planned for public spaces like parades or community picnics in the public park. They will need to prepare for supplies and law officers to help direct traffic and watch for lawbreakers. Often rights are more limited for safety in places that are crowded on a regular basis, such as public schools and courthouses.
