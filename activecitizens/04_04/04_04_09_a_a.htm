The U.S. Constitution protects the rights of people accused of crimes. It is based on the idea that people are innocent until proven guilty in a court of law. The Fifth Amendment protects a person from having to provide evidence against him or herself. The Sixth Amendment protects the right to a fair trial by jury. The Eighth Amendment protects people from cruel or unusual punishment and heavy bail or fines.

U.S. Supreme Court decisions have made specific changes to the ways these rights apply. Officers making an arrest must inform suspects of their rights because of Miranda v. Arizona. States must provide people an attorney who cannot afford one because of Gideon v. Wainwright. Juveniles have the same rights of due process as adults under the 14th Amendment because of in re Gault.
