What Determines the Value of Currency?


© 2011 AP Images

Currencies like the U.S. dollar or the Japanese yen are called "floating currencies." SupplySupply: the total amount of a good or service available for purchase and demandDemand: the total amount of a good or service that people are willing to buy determine the value of currency. When demand increases for dollars, their value goes up as well. Demand for a currency increases or decreases for many reasons. One example is the stability of a country's government. If a country is undergoing many changes, it can make foreign companies worry that they could lose money by trading with that country. Foreign companies would exchange less of their currency with that country.

To exchange two currencies, their values are measured against each other. Foreign exchange ratesForeign Exchange Rate: the amount of one currency that will be traded for another change daily based on changes in supply and demand. The foreign exchange rate is how much of one currency a person needs to buy one unit of another country's currency. For example, let's say the exchange rate is 77 Japanese yen to one U.S. dollar. That means that one U.S. dollar is worth the same as 77 Japanese yen.


Foreign Exchange Rate: 77 Japanese yen = 1 U.S. dollar




Some countries have the same name for their currencies. For example, the United States is not the only country to use the name "dollar." Canada, Australia, Hong Kong, and many others also use it. However, though they have the same name, they do not have the same value!
