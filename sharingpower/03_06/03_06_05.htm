What is Jurisdiction?

A court's jurisdictionJurisdiction: the authority of a court to hear certain types of cases will determine which cases it has the power to hear. In general, the state courts have the power to hear cases that involve laws within that state. Federal courts have jurisdiction over cases that involve federal laws. This chart summarizes what types of cases each court system will hear.

State Jurisdiction Federal Jurisdiction
Disputes about property Disputes about the Constitution
Disputes about accidents Disputes about immigration
Prosecuting someone for a crime Disputes about U.S. law
Disputes about family issues Prosecuting someone for a crime

Appellate and courts of last resort have jurisdiction over cases where a person or group appeals a lower court decision. The U.S. Supreme Court has jurisdiction over cases appealed from state supreme courts, federal courts of appeal, and in special circumstances.

An example of a situation where the U.S. Supreme Court would have original jurisdiction would be if one state government suedSued: to take legal action against someone in court to seek justice for a wrong another state government, like North Dakota versus South Dakota. The U.S. Supreme Court, however, may choose whether to hear a case appealed from a lower court. It hears only about 1 of every 100 cases requested each year. For the cases it rejects, the decision of the lower court will stand, or remain the final decision.
