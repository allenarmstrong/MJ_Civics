What Are the Levels of the Court System?

The United States has two levels in its court system:

  • state
  • federal

The U.S. ConstitutionU.S. Constitution: plan detailing the structure, functions, and powers of the government of the United States, also known as the Constitution names only one court of the judicial branchJudicial Branch: the part of government responsible for interpreting the laws and settling disputes, the U.S. Supreme CourtU.S. Supreme Court: the name for the highest court in the United States, or court of last resort, hears cases related to the U.S. Constitution. It gives CongressCongress: official name for the legislative branch of the U.S. government the power to create inferior courts, including setting rules for how the states set up their own courts as necessary.

The structure of the federal and state court systems is very similar. At both levels, trialsTrial: a formal legal process in court to settle disputes or determine the guilt or innocence of those accused of crimes will begin in the court of original jurisdictionOriginal Jurisdiction: having the right to hear a case the first time it is presented in court. States may have different courts of original jurisdiction, including districtDistrict: a geographic division for administrative or other government purposes courts and courts for specific situations like traffic tickets. States may split their district courts into separate courts to hear criminal casesCriminal Case: a lawsuit brought before a court involving someone who is accused of committing a crime and civil casesCivil Case: a lawsuit brought before a court relating to the rights of citizens, usually a dispute between citizens or groups. The federal court of original jurisdiction is the U.S. District Court.

An appealAppeal: formal request to a higher authority requesting a change of decision, such as a case to a higher court of the trial decision would go to an appellate courtAppellate Court: the jurisdiction to hear case appeals and review the decisions of lower courts, also known as a court of appeal, or court of appeals. If requested, judges in the court of last resort may choose to review the case and related laws. Many states name their highest court a "supreme court," but not all. The U.S. Supreme Court is the highest court, or the court of final authority over all other courts, state or federal. This court may hear cases from not only federal courts of appeal, but also cases appealed from a state's supreme court.
