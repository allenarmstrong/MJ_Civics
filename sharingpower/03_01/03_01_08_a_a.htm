Federalism is the division of powers among local, state, and national governments. The U.S. Constitution divides powers between the national government and the states. It delegates powers to the national government. The 10th Amendment reserves powers to the states. State constitutions determine the structure and functions of state and local governments. An example of a delegated power to the national government is the power to coin and print money. One power the Constitution has reserved to the states is the power to establish and support public schools. A concurrent, or shared, power listed in the Constitution is the power to raise and collect taxes.
