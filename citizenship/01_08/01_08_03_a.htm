Terms Text Version

Culture
Culture is the shared practices and beliefs of a group of people. People in the United States come from diverse backgrounds, but share a common American culture.

Insular Area
The United States has many insular areas, such as Guam. An insular area is U.S. territory that is not a state, a part of a state, or the District of Columbia.

Citizen
A citizen is a person who is native or naturalized to a country. Citizens have many duties and responsibilities. Most U.S. citizens are native-born.

Civic Responsibility
Civic responsibility includes the things good citizens do to promote the common good. Voting in elections and helping others in the community are examples of civic responsibilities.

Civic Duty
Civic duty includes the obligations citizens are required by law to fulfill. Paying taxes and serving on juries are examples of civic duties.

Data
Data is information, facts, or statistics. The U.S. Census Bureau collects data about Americans. The Census helps the government serve the people.

Geopolitical
Geopolitical means relating to political divisions over a geographic area. We use “geopolitical” to talk about human-made borders. An example is the imaginary lines between states.

Conservation
Conservation is the actions we take to protect Earth's resources. Good citizenship includes making choices that help protect our environment.

Citizenship
Citizenship is the legal status of being a citizen or a person’s actions and behavior as a citizen. All people, whether legal citizens or not, can act like good citizens by meeting civic responsibilities and duties.

Naturalization
Naturalization is the legal process for nonnative residents to become U.S. citizens. This is a long process. It includes residence, an application, interviews, a test, and an oath.

Community
A community is a group of people who live in the same area, have a common background, or share similar interests. You can belong to multiple communities.
