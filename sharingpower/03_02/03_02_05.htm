What Powers Do Citizens Have at the State Level?

U.S. citizensCitizen: a person who is native or naturalized to a country are most active at the local and state levels of government. Nearly half of the state constitutions provide a way for citizens to directly impact on their state.

For example, initiativeInitiative: a process in many U.S. states that allows citizens to introduce ideas for new laws to the state legislature allows citizens to draft laws and constitutional amendments for possible placement on the ballot of the next election. Voters then have the power to decide whether the initiative should pass. ReferendumReferendum: a process in many U.S. states that allows citizens to introduce ideas for changing existing laws to the state legislature enables citizens to vote on laws already passed by the state legislature. If the referendum passes, the law is removed. Recall allows voters to remove an elected official from office. Citizens might wish to recall an official accused of wrongdoing or bad management while in office.

Nationwide, citizens use the power of recall the least. In fact, not all states allow the powers of initiative, referendum, and recall. For example, Florida voters do not have the power of recall at the state level. However, they do have the powers of initiative and referendum. Between 1968 and 2006, Florida voters used initiative to amend the state constitution 22 times.
