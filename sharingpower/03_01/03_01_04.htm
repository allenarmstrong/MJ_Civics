What Is the 10th Amendment?

The 10th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution reserves powers to the states. These powers are not specifically delegated, or assigned, to the central government. Nor are these powers denied to the states. State or local governments create most of the laws Americans encounter each day.

Each of the 50 states has its own constitution, which are similar to the U.S. constitution. For example, states have a governorGovernor: the top executive of a state government, elected by the residents of that state who is the head of the executive branchExecutive Branch: the part of government responsible for enforcing the laws, just as the president is the head of the national executive branch. Each state also has a legislative branchLegislative Branch: the part of government responsible for making laws and a judicial branchJudicial Branch: the part of government responsible for interpreting the laws and settling disputes. However, state constitutions are longer and have much more detail. Elected leaders within each state helped create the state's constitution. This document is more specific about what each state government must do to serve the people within that state.

For example, states make laws to identify what must be taught in schools and homeschool settings. States require children to receive vaccines to prevent the spread of diseases. State laws also determine speed limits for roads and who can obtain a driver's license. Watch this video to learn more about state governments.
