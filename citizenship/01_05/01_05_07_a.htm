Rules of Citizenship Text Version

Review the following examples based on a person's place of birth.

Birthplace: Topeka, Kansas—People born within the states of the United States are native U.S. citizens. They have the “law of the soil,” or jus soli.

Birthplace: San Juan, Puerto Rico—People born within many territories of the United States, such as Puerto Rico, are native U.S. citizens. They have the “law of the soil,” or jus soli.

Birthplace: U.S. Naval Base at Guantánamo Bay, Cuba—The citizenship of people born on U.S. military bases depends on their parents. If at least one parent is a legal U.S. citizen and has lived in U.S. territory for at least one year before the child’s birth, the child is a native U.S. citizen. This situation is jus sanguinis, or “law of the blood.”

Birthplace: Montreal, Canada—The citizenship of people born in foreign countries depends on their parents. If at least one parent is a legal U.S. citizen and has lived in U.S. territory for at least one year before the child’s birth, the child is a native U.S. citizen. This situation is jus sanguinis, or “law of the blood.” Depending on U.S. laws and the laws of the other country, some babies may have dual citizenship. This means that two countries accept them as citizens.
