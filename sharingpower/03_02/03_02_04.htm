How Are State Governments Organized?

State governments have a similar structure to the U.S. government. They follow the principle of separation of powersSeparation of Powers: dividing government powers into legislative, executive, and judicial branches.

All 50 states have an executive branchExecutive Branch: the part of government responsible for enforcing the laws to enforce the laws, with a governorGovernor: the top executive of a state government, elected by the residents of that state at the head of the branch. Each has a legislative branchLegislative Branch: the part of government responsible for making laws that makes laws for its state. Except for Nebraska, the states have bicameral legislaturesBicameral Legislatures: a part of government that makes laws, consisting of two houses, just like the U.S. Congress. Nebraska has a unicameral legislatureUnicameral Legislature: a part of government that makes laws, consisting of one house, so only one group of people makes laws for that state.

Each state has its own judicial branchJudicial Branch: the part of government responsible for interpreting the laws and settling disputes to interpret the laws and settle criminal and civil cases. The state supreme court is the highest court within the state. It may hear appealsAppeal: formal request to a higher authority requesting a change of decision, such as a case to a higher court from lower courts. Sometimes cases heard by a state supreme court are appealed to the U.S. Supreme Court if there is a possibility that the state law related to the case is in conflict with the U.S. Constitution.
