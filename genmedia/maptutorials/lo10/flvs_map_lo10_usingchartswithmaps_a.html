Screen 4 of 7:

Image of a map of the 2008 Presidential Election Results, state-level. McCain ridings in red, Obama ridings in blue, various size gray dots in each state. Approximately half of the country is red and half blue.

This map shows not only the state election results but also the electoral vote for each state. Cartographers use a dot or symbol to represent one or a group of items, like a single electoral vote.

Cartographers combine data with maps to show relationships between the data and the geography. You can compare additional data charts with maps for more information. Maps can show counts, rates, and densities.

Screen 5 of 7:

Image of a pie-graph of the 2008 U.S. Registered Voters by Political Party, Democrat - 72 million, Republican - 55 million, Independant - 42 million; and an image of a partial map of the United States of U.S. Electoral Vote 2008 and Registered Voter Party Affiliation by State. A pie chart of different ratios on each state.

Rate data is a ratio of one count to another, like the number of registered voters for each political party. Cartographers relate this information to geography by displaying it on a map, often using pie charts or bar graphs within the map.

Screen 6 of 7:

Image of a U.S. Population Density Map. Each state is shown in a shade of green indicating the level the population per square mile per state using 2009 Census figures.

Density data shows a relationship between a count and a distance, like the average number of people living in a square mile. Density is an average across an area but would not be correct for every neighborhood in a city or state. Therefore, cartographers usually use a choropleth map like this one to display density.

Screen 7 of 7:

Image of a young girl thinking.

When you analyze chart data with maps, ask yourself these questions.

  • What information is the map giving you in the title, the map key or legend, and the scale?
  • What information is the chart giving you?
  • How do the chart and the map relate to each other?
  • How might the information be misleading?
  • What is the cartographer trying to prove?

What Can a Map Tell Us Besides Location

Lesson 4 of 5:

Image of map of trade between Africa, Europe, North America, and the Caribbean during American colonization. The map is shaded in different colors for tropical rainforest, subtropical rainforest, temperate broadleaf forest, temperate steppe, grass savanna, tree savanna, semiarid desert, or arid desert. There are also arrows indicating direction of trade between areas.

You can draw many conclusions from this map. Cartographers combine data with maps to study history as well as current issues.

This map represents the triangular trade between Africa, Europe, and North America/Caribbean during the time of American colonization. From this map you can:

  • learn not only which regions produced what products but who bought them
  • draw conclusions from the vegetation information about why each area may have specialized in certain products
    Note: On the map, the direction ships carrying these products followed to estimate the distance is indicated.
  • draw conclusions about the lives and work of people in each area at the time
  • ask questions that will lead you to learn more about the history of each region
