How Does Lawmaking Affect Governments?

The U.S. ConstitutionU.S. Constitution: plan detailing the structure, functions, and powers of the government of the United States, also known as the Constitution creates a government system for the nation based on federalismFederalism: division of powers among local, state, and national governments. With three main levels of government, multiple lawmaking groups affect the American people, as well as the other levels of government.

Ideas for new laws are usually called "bills." When a law is proposed, legislators will discuss its intended purpose and any potential effects it might cause. They will debate the specific language in the bill and perfect it before taking a vote on whether to pass it into law. If passed, people must follow the law, and the executive branch must enforce it.

Under the Constitution, federal laws are supreme. That means that if a state or local law is in conflict with federal law, the federal law will be the standard used in courts. As a result, federal laws often have a great impact on state and local governments and the laws they pass. Local and state governments must follow federal laws just like individual citizens.
