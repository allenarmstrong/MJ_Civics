What Does the Verdict Mean?


© 2011 AP Images

In a civil trial, the jury will rule that a person is either liableLiable: having legal responsibility for costs or damages or not liable to the other. If liable, the person or group will owe compensation to the other.

You may have heard that in a criminal trial, people tried before a court are "innocent until proven guilty." In the U.S. court system, it is the job of the prosecutionProsecution: the lawyers representing the people or a government body in a criminal trial to gather evidence and prove guilt. A person on trial does not have to prove he or she is innocent. We accept that they are, unless the government proves otherwise.

Sometimes people confuse the terms "not guilty" and "innocent." A jury does not rule a person innocent. Instead, they would say the person is not guilty. An innocent person is completely without fault. This is impossible to prove. A verdict of not guilty, however, could mean that the person did not commit the crime. It might even mean the crime never happened, the wrong person was arrested, or that the prosecution failed to prove its case.
