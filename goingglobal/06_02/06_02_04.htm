Why Is "Per Capita" Data Important?

Many people relate a higher GDP and less crime with a better standard of living. However, it is important to look at many pieces of data. A single number can be misleading because countries vary a great deal in size and population. Data "per capita" gives a better idea of daily life. Many pieces of data, such as GDP, are labeled "per capita" or "rate" to show the data by each person or a certain number of people.

For example, the U.S. GDP for 2010 was over 14.6 trillion dollars. It's higher than most countries but does not tell us much about standard of living. We compare it with other data to learn more. For example, we can say it was higher than Qatar's GDP of 129.5 billion dollars. Yet countries differ in size and number of people. When you divide GDP by the population, the new figure is called GDP per capita. The United States is ranked 9th in the world for GDP per capita. However, Qatar, despite its much lower GDP, has the highest GDP per capita in the world. Qatar has a lower population than the United States.

Another example is airplane flights. The United States leads this figure by far, with over eight million registered take-offs in 2005. The Bahamas had only about 29,400 departures. The United States is a larger country with a larger population. More U.S. flights makes sense. However, when you compare the data per capita, you see a different story.
