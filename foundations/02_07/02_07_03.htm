Why Is the Bill of Rights Important?

The Bill of Rights is the name given to the first 10 amendmentsAmendment: a formal change to the U.S. Constitution to the Constitution. It contains a list of individual rights that Americans have and that government must respect. The First Amendment is probably the most famous. This amendment protects a person's rights to free speechFree Speech: right to express opinions in public, pressPress: all the people involved in gathering and reporting the news, assemblyAssembly: the coming together of people for a common purpose, petitionPetition: a formal request, or to make a formal request, and religious expressionReligious Expression: the communication of religious beliefs, thoughts, or feelings. Political expressionPolitical Expression: the communication of thoughts, beliefs, or feelings related to government or the actions of government through freedom of speech is a protected right for Americans. When the Tinkers took their case to court, they argued that the armbands they wore to school were a form of political expression protected by the First Amendment.

Early leaders of the U.S. government wanted to limit government so that it could not take away rights contained in the Bill of Rights. The Bill of Rights is important because it gives extra protection to these rights, such as freedom of speech. In many Supreme Court cases, including the Tinkers', the justices base their ruling on one or more of these amendments, other laws, and prior cases.
