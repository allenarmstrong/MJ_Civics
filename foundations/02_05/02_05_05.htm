What Are the Three Branches of Government?

The Constitution divides the powers of government into three branches. The legislativeLegislative Branch: the part of government responsible for making laws, executiveExecutive Branch: the part of government responsible for enforcing the laws, and judicialJudicial Branch: the part of government responsible for interpreting the laws and settling disputes branches each have specific structures, and functions. In addition, the Constitution provides requirements for citizens seeking positions working in each branch.

Note that the executive branch has many people besides the president. They work in different positions. The president is the leader of this branch at the federal level. Yet many people work with and for the president daily to help carry out the laws.

Examine this chart to learn about the structure of the branches.

Legislative Branch

Article I (One) "Congress"

  • Makes the laws ("legislates")
  • Made up of the House of Representatives and the Senate
  • Voters choose representatives and senators
  • House of Representatives has two-year terms
  • A representative must be at least 25 years of age, a citizen for at least seven years, and living in the state that they represent
  • Senate has six-year terms
  • A senator must be at least 30 years of age, a citizen for at least nine years, and living in the state that they represent
  • Representatives and senators may be re-elected
  • 435 representatives divided among the states by population, and three representatives for the federal capital, Washington D. C.
  • 100 sentators, two from each state
  • Meets in the U.S. Capitol Building

Executive Branch

Article II (Two) "President"

  • Carries out the laws
  • Made up of the President, Vice President, the Cabinet, and other heads of federal agencies and their staff
  • The president and vice president are elected to four-year terms
  • The president (and vice president) must be at least 35 years of age, a natural-born citizen, and living in the United States for at least 14 years
  • The Electoral College is the voting system for choosing the president
  • The president may not serve more than two terms (22nd Amendment)
  • The president appoints Cabinet members and the Senate confirms them
  • The vice president can break tied votes in the Senate
  • Meets in the White House

Judicial Branch

Article III (Three) "Supreme Court"

  • Interprets the laws and Constitution
  • The Supreme Court, is the only court created by the Constitution
  • Nine Supreme Court justices (one Chief Justice and eight associate justices)
  • Supreme Court justices serve for life--until death, retirement, or removal by Congress
  • The president appoints a justice when there is an opening and the Senate confirms the person chosen
  • Meets in the U.S. Supreme Court Building
