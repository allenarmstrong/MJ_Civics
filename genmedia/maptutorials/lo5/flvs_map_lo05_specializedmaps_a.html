Lesson 1 of 7:

Screen 1 of 2:

This animation shows a series of cartograms, which are maps that show statistics as they relate between land areas. Notice that as the maps change by year, North America increases in size. This is because the North American Population increased as a portion of the total world population. Cartograms distort land shape on purpose to display information.
(Image titled in the bottom left corner "Share of the World Population". Image depicts the world map, with North America and Europe positioned at the top edge. The image is interactive and changes with time. Beginning with year one, we see North, Central, and South America depicted as very thin land masses compared to present day form. Europe, Asia and Africa are also seen but are depicted similar to their present day form. As year one fades out year 1500 fades in and we can see North, Central, and South America increase in size. Europe, and Africa increase in size slightly and Asia slightly decreases. Next in 1900 we see again that the America's increase in size and we see Europe increase in size quite noticeably. Africa and Asia decrease in size slightly. Finally in 1960 we see growth from all areas on the map except Asia, and Europe which decreases in size quite noticeably. The image then fades into screen 2 showing additional text)

Historical and Dot Density Maps

Lesson 2 of 7:

Examine the image to learn about historical maps, which represent Earth’s features at a previous point in time, and dot-density maps, which show the frequency of a specific item or event over a geographic area.
(Image is a map of the United States, the map is titled “Cropland Harvested Acreage, 1949”, and a yellow circle is placed here to show a “hot spot”. There is also a key stating 1 dot is equal to 25,000 acres. The maps land mass is colored green, with the exception of a number of black dots covering much of the central and northeastern states and spread thinly across the rest of the states. A yellow circle marks another hot spot where the black dots seem to be the thickest, just to the northwest border of Iowa)

Rollover the hotspots in the image for more information:
Hot Spot Description:
Cropland Harvested Acreage, 1949: Notice a cartographer produced this map in the past. Historical Maps are useful for learning about an area and future planning in many industries. Historical Maps are based on a previous point in time.
Hotspot by dots on map: This is a dot-density map showing the frequency of a specific item or event over a geographic area. This area is very dense with dots indicating many more acres of land harvested here than other areas.

More on Historical Maps

Lesson 3 of 7:

When we study history, we may be interested in maps made by past cartographers. These maps are historical artifacts produced at the time the map represents. Historical maps made in the past often contain many inaccuracies. They teach us about how people viewed their world and the information they had.
(Image is a historical map titled “1950 Map of Florida. The map looks nothing like present day Florida. Here we can see that the cartographer shows Florida as a “V”, and is not as large as it should be in height. There is a body of water in the northern area of Florida. We can also see 3 islands to the right of Florida, which are not named)

Choropleth Maps


Lesson 5 of 7:
Choropleth maps use color to display information such as climate and weather information. Examine the image for an example.
(Image is a map titled “Choropleth Map of India’s Climate”. A map key shows the following descriptions; Pink – Highland, Aqua – Humid Subtropical, Light Green – Tropical Wet, Dark Green – Tropical Wet, Tan – Arid, Orange – Semi-Arid. Four of the main states are titled; Rajasthan in the northwest, Assam in the northeast, Madhya Pradesh in the center, and Kerala on the southwest coast. Rajasthan is mostly tan with some orange borders to the east. North of this state we see some pink and aqua, making up part of Madhya Pradesh, the rest of this state to the south is mostly light green with a strip of orang extending from the center of the state to the southernmost tip. Kerala is to the southwest of Madhya Pradesh and is mostly green. Finally in the north east we find Assam and it is mostly aqua, with exception of some pink in the north.)

Rollover the hotspots in the image for more information:
Hot spot in Rajasthan: This map uses color to show the extent of climate areas in India. Notice that arid or dry areas, like the state of Rajasthan, are covered in tan.
Hot spot in Kerala: Most of India is very humid. For example, the state of Kerala has a wet, tropical climate, and therefore it is colored dark green. Kerala often experiences flooding during the rainy monsoon months of June to October.

GIS and GPS Mapping


Lesson 6 of 7:
GPS, or Global Positioning System, uses satellite technology developed for the military to locate a precise coordinate point for someone or something. GIS, or Global Information System, is sets of descriptive or scientific data for a place.
Cartographers can use GPS and GIS separately or together to create accurate maps useful in many areas. Drivers, pilots, hiker, and boaters use GPS to navigate and avoid collisions. Researchers and travellers use GIS to study scientific of feature information for a place.
(Image of a map showing land, water and roadways. Ocala National Forrest is labeled in the upper left corner, at the bottom of the map a roadway labeled as Country Rd. 445 moving diagonally from the middle of the map to the right. A location is pointed out on the map by a black dot called by Alexander Springs. Another roadway moves up from Country Rd. 445 and loops back on itself, here there is a red pin. A white info bubble appears above the pin and states “Alexander Springs Lat. 29.07889 Long. -81.58.” The bubble also shows another image of the location itself, a photograph. The location is a light blue body of water, surrounded by trees and foliage, with white sand on one shore. )
GPS tells us “where.” GPS gives the latitude and longitude coordinate point, or absolute location, for Alexander Springs on this map. GIS, on the other hand, tells us “what.” GIS would tell us about Alexander Springs’ characteristics, such as water depth or native plants.
